Reading a Scientific Paper

READING A SCIENTIFIC PAPER

Ann Nordmeyer (Southern New Hampshire University) & Michael C. Frank (Stanford)

 

[This handout is designed for students learning to read papers in psychology and the behavioral sciences.]

The biggest difference between reading the scientific literature and reading other kinds of documents is that you don’t read a paper from beginning to end like a novel! Reading from start to finish is not useful if you don't extract the information that you need; likewise, you will see people who don’t read start to finish but extract all of the useful information much more quickly.

 

IN CLASS READING

 

[Take 5 minutes and read the assigned paper for this class. Five minutes is probably not enough time! And that’s the whole point.]

FIRST READING – POINTS TO RETAIN

 

The most important parts of reading a scientific paper is being able to extract:

  1. The primary question of the article,
  2. The claim that is made in response to the question, and
  3. How the data support that primary claim.

Although there are many parts of the article that you will need in read order to verify the claim (e.g. methods, control studies and analyses, etc.), these are the major pieces of information we take away from the article. They are what we will summarize in a review, or recount to another person who wants to know about the paper.

Exercise 1: Put down the paper and write three sentences: the question, the claim, and the data.

[Now reflect: Are you happy with how you did writing these? What did you not encode about the paper?]

 

Exercise 2: Pick up the paper and take another 5 minutes to write a new version of these three things.

[Share some of these with the class.]

 

Exercise 3: Take 2 minutes and find a sentence in the paper that best captures these three things.

[Check with the class - did everyone find the same sentences? If you can find those sentences then you don’t always have to read the whole paper! Sometimes (but not always) this vital information is in the abstract of a paper. Whether it is will depend on how well the abstract is written, how complex the paper is, etc. But often you will have to dig somewhat deeper. Look especially at the last paragraph of the Intro and first paragraph of the General Discussion.]

 

Exercise 4: Take 1 minute and find the graph, diagram, or table you’d point to in order to convey the primary finding of the paper.

[Not every paper has a “key figure” but many do – and that’s what you would typically use if you were presenting that paper to a class or a lab meeting.]

 

[50 minute class ends here]

IN DEPTH READING – ASSESSING THE EXPERIMENT

 

Reading the Methods

 

Engaging with whether the claims are supported by the experiments in a psychology paper requires the reader to understand the methods of the experiment, ideally in such a way that the reader can imagine being in the experiment.

 

Exercise 5: Put down the paper. Take 3 minutes to write a few lines about what was it like to be in an individual trial in the experiment. (Describe this in the second person, e.g. “you hear a sentence.”) Now, what were the different kinds of trials (e.g., different conditions)?

 

Alternative Explanations

 

Exercise 6: What is the controversial claim? Now that we are talking about the paper in depth, we can try and summarize the claim quickly and efficiently.

 

Exercise 7: Take 3 minutes and choose an alternative explanation for this claim. Write one to two lines explaining what the authors do to rule out this claim.

[80 minute class ends here]
